The global music industry, having navigated the turbulent transition from physical sales to digital access, now finds itself at the precipice of a second structural crisis. This crisis is not one of consumption—which has reached historic heights—but of distribution. At the center of this debate is the competition between the established pro-rata distribution model and the emerging user-centric alternative. While the former has facilitated the industry's recovery and the rise of a new class of global superstars, it has simultaneously institutionalized a set of economic incentives that favor market concentration and invite large-scale fraudulent exploitation. The fundamental architecture of the "Big Pool" system, utilized by market leaders like Spotify and Apple Music, creates a zero-sum environment where legitimate creative value is systematically diluted by artificial engagement and the strategic manipulation of total platform market share.1

Section 1

The Economic Infrastructure of the Pro-Rata Model

The pro-rata or "streamshare" model is the foundational accounting mechanism of the modern streaming era. Its operation is predicated on the aggregation of all subscription and advertising revenue within a specific geographic territory and price tier into a single, centralized royalty pool.1 Before any artist or songwriter receives compensation, the platform—the Digital Service Provider (DSP)—retains an administrative share, typically approximately 30%, to cover operational overhead, research and development, and corporate margin.2 The remaining 70% is then allocated to the rights holders, a category that encompasses record labels, music publishers, collection societies, and digital distributors.5

The distribution of this 70% share is dictated by a simple but impactful fraction: an artist's total monthly streams divided by the total number of qualifying streams across the entire platform during that same period.2 This methodology ensures that every stream is, in theory, equal in value, yet in practice, the value of an individual stream is a derived and highly volatile metric. Because the total number of streams (the denominator) is theoretically infinite, but the revenue pool (the numerator) is finite and relatively stable month-to-month, an increase in platform-wide consumption directly reduces the value of every individual stream.

The Mathematical Foundation of Streamshare

Pro-Rata Revenue Formula Revenue(artisti) = (Streams(artisti) ÷ Σ All Platform Streams) × Net Revenue Pool
  • · Streams(artisti) — payment-relevant streams (plays exceeding 30 seconds) for artist i
  • · Σ All Platform Streams — summation of all qualifying streams across the platform for that month
  • · Net Revenue Pool — gross revenue after taxes and transaction fees are deducted2

This framework creates a mathematical environment where artists are in constant, direct competition with one another. If one artist increases their stream count, they do not just earn more; they simultaneously reduce the percentage share available to every other artist on the platform. Every single point of market share in the current streaming economy is estimated to be worth approximately $200 million USD.11

The Tiered Distribution of Royalties

The 70% share of revenue that flows out of the platform is further subdivided into two primary copyright buckets: recording royalties and publishing royalties. The allocation between these two is historically weighted toward the recording, which represents the physical (or digital) master file.8

Royalty Stream % of 70% Share Final Recipient Mechanism
Recording (Master) ~80% Record Labels, Performers, Distributors Paid directly to labels or distributors8
Publishing (Song) ~20% Songwriters, Composers, Publishers Paid to PROs/Mechanical Societies8

For artists signed to major labels, the recording share revenue is subjected to further deductions for recoupment of advances, marketing costs, and the label's own profit share, which can often leave the artist with only 15% to 25% of the gross streaming revenue generated by their work.8 The publishing share is even more complex, moving through Performing Rights Organizations (PROs) and mechanical rights societies before reaching songwriters, often with significant delays and administrative deductions.8

Section 2

The Beneficiaries: Scale, Superstars, and Cross-Subsidization

The pro-rata model is, by its very design, a system of concentration. It rewards scale above all other metrics, creating a "superstar effect" that has only intensified as the platform catalogs have grown to exceed 100 million tracks.4

The 1% Phenomenon and Market Concentration

The reality of the streamshare economy is one of extreme Pareto distribution. As of 2025, analysis of major label rosters reveals that the top 1% of artists account for approximately 90% of total platform streams.12 This concentration enables elite artists to negotiate favorable royalty shares that can exceed 25% of gross revenue, further widening the gap between the industry's upper echelon and the "long tail" of independent creators.12

Spotify's own data highlights this trend. In 2024, while more than 12,500 artists earned over $100,000 from the platform, only 1,500 earned over $1 million, and a mere 70 artists surpassed the $10 million mark.6 While these numbers represent a 600% increase in top-tier earners since 2017, they must be viewed against the backdrop of a platform that hosts millions of creators, the vast majority of whom receive economically irrelevant revenue.2

The Logic of Cross-Subsidization

Perhaps the most significant criticism of the pro-rata model is the phenomenon of cross-subsidization. Under this system, the subscription fees of individual users are not directed to the artists they personally listen to. Instead, the money is pooled and distributed based on global or regional popularity.1 This creates a situation where a "casual" listener, who pays $10.99 a month but only streams ten songs by a niche classical artist, is effectively paying for the streams of a "heavy" user who listens to thousands of songs by a pop superstar.2

User Type Monthly Fee Streams Royalty Contribution Logic
Low-Consumption (Casual) $10.99 10 Majority of fee subsidizes the global pool2
High-Consumption (Heavy) $10.99 10,000 Captures 1,000× the share of the casual user2

This dynamic is not merely an accounting quirk; it is a transfer of wealth from low-consumption users to heavy-streaming users. Because heavy users generate a disproportionately large number of streams, they command a larger share of the royalty pool. Consequently, the artists listened to by heavy users—often those in pop, hip-hop, or "lean-back" functional genres—receive a portion of the subscription fees from every user on the platform, including those who have never played their music.2

Section 3

The Prize for Fraud: Incentives for Artificial Engagement

The zero-sum nature of the pro-rata pool creates a profound economic incentive for fraud. If a malicious actor can generate a massive volume of streams at a cost lower than the resulting royalty payout, they can effectively "tax" the entire music industry, diverting funds from legitimate creators to themselves.3

The Dilution of Human Value

Every stream generated by a bot directly dilutes the value of every stream generated by a human listener. Because the total royalty pool is fixed, the introduction of fraudulent streams into the denominator of the streamshare fraction reduces the payout for every legitimate artist on the platform.3

If bot traffic accounts for just 5% of total platform streams, it represents an annual diversion of approximately $550 million USD from honest creators to fraudulent actors.11 In 2024 alone, the total revenue lost to streaming fraud worldwide was estimated to be as high as $2 billion.16

The "prize for fraud" is particularly attractive because of the low barrier to entry. On digital platforms, music is virtually free to access for subscribers, and the mass creation of fake accounts can be achieved with minimal investment. If a bot operator can generate enough streams to exceed the cost of the subscription and the technical overhead, the remaining royalty payout is pure profit.10

Technical Vulnerabilities and the Feedback Loop

Fraudsters exploit several specific technical gaps in the pro-rata model:

  • Decentralized Botnets: Modern bot farms use distributed infrastructure to decentralize IP addresses, making it difficult for traffic monitoring tools to identify coordinated patterns.16
  • Human-Emulating Behavior: Advanced bots are programmed to simulate human listening patterns, such as pausing between tracks, creating playlists, and varying listening hours, making them nearly indistinguishable from real users.16
  • Algorithmic Distortion: Fraudulent streams create a false perception of popularity, which can trick the platform's recommendation algorithms into promoting the fraudulent tracks to legitimate users, creating a feedback loop of unearned exposure and revenue.16
Section 4

Anatomy of a High-Volume Fraud Scheme: The Michael Smith Case

The prosecution of Michael Smith in 2024 provides a definitive case study in the mechanics of large-scale streaming fraud. Smith, a musician from North Carolina, allegedly orchestrated a scheme that defrauded rights holders of over $10 million in royalties between 2017 and 2024.17

Case Study — Michael Smith Streaming Fraud Operation

The operation was built on three pillars: AI-generated repertoire (hundreds of thousands of fabricated tracks), a distributed bot infrastructure of up to 10,000 accounts masked by VPNs, and strategic volume management to stay below platform detection thresholds.17 Smith was flagged by a distributor in 2018 but successfully denied wrongdoing, allowing the scheme to continue for another six years.17

Section 5

The User-Centric Alternative: Breaking the Zero-Sum Game

The primary alternative to the pro-rata system is the user-centric royalty model. In this framework, the net subscription fee of each individual user is distributed only to the artists that specific user listened to.1

The Mechanics of User-Centric Distribution

Under a user-centric model, there is no shared pool of revenue that artists compete for at the global level. Instead, each user creates their own "micro-pool." If a user pays a $10.99 monthly fee and listens to only five songs, the entirety of that fee (after the platform's 30% cut) is divided among the rights holders of those five songs.1

User-Centric Revenue Formula Revenue(artisti) = Σu ∈ U [ (Streams(u, i) ÷ Total Streams(u)) × Fee(u) ]
  • · Streams(u, i) — streams from user u for artist i
  • · Total Streams(u) — total streams by user u that month
  • · Fee(u) — distributable portion of user u's subscription fee10

Immunity to Large-Scale Fraud

The most profound advantage of the user-centric model is its inherent resistance to the "prize for fraud." In a pro-rata system, an artist can profit from faking a new user by more than the subscription cost, because those fake streams capture value from the global pool. In a user-centric system, an artist can never gain more from a fake user than the subscription fee that user paid.10

Because the DSP still retains its 30% cut, a fraudster paying for 10,000 subscriptions to stream their own music would effectively be paying the platform for the privilege of stealing from themselves. This creates a state of "single-user fraud-proofness," mathematically eliminating the economic incentive for high-volume bot farms.10

Winners and Losers: The CNM Study

Empirical research has provided a clear picture of who would benefit from a shift to user-centricity. A study by the Centre National de la Musique (CNM), using data from Spotify and Deezer, found that the transition would primarily impact the most successful artists.13

Artist Tier (by streams) Deezer Impact Spotify Impact
Top 10 −17.2% −12.5%
Ranked 11–100 +0.9% +0.9%
Ranked 1,001–10,000 +0.4% +0.4%
Top 0.4% (Elite) revenue share Would drop from 10% to 5.6%15

The "losers" in this model are the superstars whose reach is wide but whose engagement may be shallow or driven by passive, heavy-streaming users. The "winners" are the niche, independent, and professional artists who possess highly loyal fanbases. Genres like jazz, classical, and traditional world music—which are often excluded from the pro-rata "Big Pool" rewards—would see a significant increase in their share of the revenue.4

Section 6

Modernizing the Royalty System: Spotify's 2024 Reforms

In response to the mounting pressure regarding fraud and pool dilution, Spotify introduced a series of "modernization" policies that went live on April 1, 2024. These changes were designed to drive an additional $1 billion toward "emerging and professional artists" over a five-year period.23

The 1,000-Stream Threshold

The most significant change was the introduction of a minimum streaming threshold for royalty eligibility. Tracks must now reach at least 1,000 streams in the previous 12 months to generate any recorded royalties.5 Spotify's rationale is twofold: eliminating micro-payments (tens of millions of tracks were generating an average of $0.03 per month, amounts that fell below distributor withdrawal minimums) and reallocating approximately $40 million per year in sub-threshold revenue to qualifying tracks.23,11

While Spotify frames this as a benefit to "honest, hardworking artists," critics and researchers argue that the policy may fail to deter fraud while simultaneously harming niche creators whose annual stream counts hover just below the threshold.3

Penalizing "Noise" and Functional Audio

The modernization also targeted "noise" recordings—white noise, rain sounds, and nature recordings—which had become a hotbed for gaming the pro-rata system. Fraudsters would cut noise tracks into 30-second segments to maximize their stream count.23 The new policy implemented a minimum track length of two minutes for functional noise recordings and a reduction in the value of noise streams, making them worth a fraction of a music stream.11

Financial Deterrents for Artificial Streaming

In a major shift toward institutional accountability, Spotify began charging labels and distributors a fee for every track where "flagrant artificial streaming" is detected.23 This moves the burden of fraud prevention from the platform alone to the entities responsible for distributing the content, creating a financial disincentive for those who might otherwise turn a blind eye to suspicious activity in their catalogs.23

Section 7

Hybridity and the Rise of the Artist-Centric Model

As the debate between pro-rata and user-centricity continues, several platforms have begun implementing "artist-centric" or hybrid models that attempt to capture the benefits of both systems while mitigating their flaws.

Deezer — Dual-Boost System

Launched in 2023 in collaboration with Universal Music Group, Deezer's artist-centric system rewards intentionality over passive consumption.11 Artists with more than 1,000 streams from 500 unique listeners per month receive a "double boost," meaning their streams are weighted twice as heavily in the royalty pool.24 If a listener actively searches for a track or artist, that stream is doubled again.24

Crucially, Deezer implemented a 1,000-stream cap per user per month. Streams beyond this limit are weighted at a lower rate (0.5 streams apiece), effectively reducing the power of a single account to distort the global pool and neutralizing the impact of high-volume fraud.24

SoundCloud — Fan-Powered Royalties

SoundCloud has been the most prominent advocate for true user-centric distribution through its "Fan-Powered Royalties" program.14 Unlike the pro-rata model, SoundCloud's system ties royalties directly to listener engagement, allowing artists to benefit from their specific fanbase's dedication rather than their global market share.25

By 2025, SoundCloud was expanding this ecosystem by offering artists 100% of their royalties from distribution to other platforms and integrating direct fan monetization tools like merch sales and on-demand vinyl.25 This strategy recognizes that in a world of infinite supply, the value lies in the "superfan" relationship rather than the commodity stream.25

Section 8

The Convergence of AI, Fraud, and Gross Margin

The future of the streaming royalty debate is increasingly intertwined with the rise of generative AI. The pro-rata system's reward for "infinite supply at near-zero unit cost" creates a vacuum that is rapidly being filled by AI-generated content.3

The "Less Than Zero" Effect

As human-created tracks are substituted with synthetic "sound-alikes," noise, or functional audio, the platforms' royalty liability is often reduced. AI-generated tracks can be counted just long enough to dilute the total royalty pool for a given period before they are detected and removed—only to be replaced by a fresh batch of synthetic content.3

From an investor perspective, this dilution is a lever for expanding gross margins. By making the artist an "externality" and steering demand toward low-or-no-royalty inventory (such as commissioned AI or public-domain tracks), platforms can keep their revenue intact while reducing their largest variable cost: royalties.3

The Sustainability of the $11 Billion Injection

Despite the challenges of fraud and dilution, the scale of the streaming economy continues to grow. In 2025, Spotify's annual payout reached $11 billion, contributing to a cumulative all-time total of over $70 billion.6 This growth is fueled by geographic expansion into emerging markets and a "long runway of untapped pricing power".28 However, the sustainability of this model depends on its perceived fairness. Organizations like the Union of Musicians and Allied Workers (UMAW) continue to advocate for a "penny-per-stream" minimum and a move toward user-centricity, arguing that the current system's opacity protects platforms at the expense of creators.15

Section 9

Conclusions and Strategic Implications

The comparative analysis of pro-rata versus user-centric royalty models reveals a fundamental tension between the industry's desire for aggregate scale and the individual artist's need for equitable compensation.

Summary of Findings

  1. Pro-Rata Systemic Bias The current pro-rata model institutionalizes a superstar-favoring environment where 1% of artists capture 90% of streams. It relies on a cross-subsidization logic that diverts revenue from casual, niche-supporting listeners to the global pop stars streamed by heavy users.4
  2. The Prize for Fraud The pro-rata model's zero-sum nature creates a multi-billion-dollar incentive for artificial stream inflation. High-volume schemes exploit the inability of DSPs to verify billions of streams in real-time, resulting in a direct "tax" on legitimate creators.3
  3. User-Centric Fraud Proofing The user-centric model provides a structural defense against large-scale bot fraud by capping the revenue any single account can generate at the level of its own subscription fee. This mathematically eliminates the profitability of high-volume bot farms.10
  4. Distributive Fairness A transition to user-centricity would result in a significant redistribution of revenue (up to 17%) from the top 10 superstars to the "long tail" of professional and niche artists, particularly benefiting genres with dedicated but lower-volume fanbases.13
  5. Modernization and Thresholds Spotify's 2024 policy changes, including the 1,000-stream threshold, represent a pragmatic attempt to declutter the pool but may fail to address the underlying economic incentives for sophisticated fraud.18

Actionable Recommendations

For Digital Service Providers

Identity & Hybrid Architecture

  • Implement "Know Your Artist" (KYA) identity verification to prevent mass creation of fraudulent profiles.17
  • Adopt qualified pro-rata or hybrid models—as seen with Deezer and SoundCloud—that preserve pool simplicity while introducing user-centric fraud safeguards.24
For Rights Holders and Labels

Superfan Monetization

  • Shift focus from defending market share through volume to cultivating "Superfan" monetization strategies.
  • As streaming revenue plateaus in mature markets, the ability to monetize the top 2% of a fanbase through direct-to-fan offerings will become the primary differentiator between success and obsolescence.25

The economic infrastructure of the streaming era is not static. The ongoing competition between the pro-rata and user-centric models will ultimately define the industry's ability to support a diverse and sustainable creative ecosystem in the face of AI-driven automation and organized digital fraud.3
